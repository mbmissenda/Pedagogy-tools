Two Ways to Change a System

You have an evidence-informed integrative-health project you want to move forward inside an institution that wasn't built for it. There are two very different ways to think about the work ahead — one engineered, one brokered. Both are useful, in different moments, and both are stronger when anchored in the evidence you've appraised — or the evidence you're still chasing. This tool helps you hold your project up to each way of thinking and hear where your reasoning is strong or thin.

A separate diagnostic — Why it Stalled — is offered alongside the two ways as a companion. It's a way to learn from a project (yours, a colleague's, or a case from the literature) that ran out of fuel, useful as a check on your current effort before it does the same.

How to use this: Tell us a little about you, your project, and where you are with the evidence. Then work through whichever way of thinking fits your moment — and visit the diagnostic when you want to pressure-test what you've built. Each panel opens with a short reference you can keep open while you think. After each prompt there's a quick check-in — not a grade, a mirror. Your work saves automatically, and you can carry it with you using the buttons at the bottom.

The Blueprint

Lens: Intervention Mapping (Fernandez et al., 2019)

This lens assumes that change is engineered. Before you act, you specify the problem, the people, the determinants, the theory-based methods, and the evaluation. The work upfront is heavy on purpose: a well-mapped intervention is harder to derail and easier to defend. Hold your project up to this lens and answer in your own words.

Reference How this framework works

Intervention Mapping builds an intervention backward from the problem. Before you decide what to do, you specify what has to change, in whom, and why — and on what theoretical basis. The prompts below ask for each of those layers in turn. Use this reference if a term feels unfamiliar.

Logic model of the problem
A left-to-right map. On the right: the quality of life affected. To its left: the health problem. To its left: the behaviors and environmental conditions that sustain the problem. Furthest left: the personal and environmental determinants that cause those behaviors and conditions.
Determinants
The modifiable factors behind a behavior or condition. Personal determinants live in the person — knowledge, attitudes, beliefs, skills, self-efficacy, outcome expectations. Environmental determinants live at four nested levels: interpersonal, organizational, community, and societal/policy.
Change objectives
A change objective pairs a determinant with what a specific actor must do, learn, or believe differently for that determinant to shift. Written by actor — "patients will…", "clinic schedulers will…", "department chairs will…" — and specific enough that you could tell whether it happened.
Theory-based methods and practical applications
A method is the theoretical mechanism of change drawn from behavioral and social science (modeling, persuasion, environmental restructuring, and others). A practical application is the concrete activity participants encounter that delivers the method — a workshop, a coaching call, a redesigned form. Each method has parameters of effectiveness: the conditions under which it actually works. Honoring those parameters is what makes a method translate.
Adoption and implementation
A useful intervention that isn't taken up isn't a change. Adoption is who chooses the intervention; implementation is who runs it day to day. Both have their own determinants and may require their own change objectives.
Evaluation
Four layers, each measuring something different. Process: did the intervention happen as planned? Effect: did the targeted determinants shift? Behavioral: did the behaviors change? Health: did the problem move?

For depth: Fernandez, M. E., et al. (2019). Implementation Mapping: Using Intervention Mapping to develop implementation strategies. Frontiers in Public Health, 7, 209.

The problem and its logic

Sketch the chain from determinants → behaviors and environmental conditions → health problem → quality of life.

  • What behaviors and environmental conditions sustain that problem? Which of those will your project actually try to change?
  • If you drew a logic model on a napkin right now, what would be on the left and what would be on the right?

Determinants worth targeting

For each behavior or environmental condition: what individual-level and environmental-level factors cause or maintain it?

  • At the individual level — what knowledge, beliefs, skills, self-efficacy, or outcome expectations feed the behavior?
  • At the environmental levels — interpersonal, organizational, community, policy — what conditions feed it?
  • Of all the determinants you just listed, which are changeable? Which carry the most weight? How do you know — or what would you need to find out?

4

Theory-based methods and practical applications

A method is the theoretical mechanism (e.g., modeling, persuasion, environmental restructuring). A practical application is what the participant actually encounters.

  • For each change objective, what behavior-change theory or method has evidence of working on that kind of determinant?
  • What are the parameters of effectiveness for that method — the conditions under which it actually works — and how will you respect them in your application?
  • What is the concrete thing the participant will see, hear, or do? A workshop? A coaching call? A redesigned form?

5

Adoption and implementation

A well-designed intervention that no one adopts is not a change.

  • Who has to adopt this — not just consent, but actually use it? What would they need to see, hear, or experience to choose adoption?
  • Who has to implement it day to day? What's in it for them, and what's it going to cost them?
  • What channels reach each of those groups?

6

Evaluation

Process, effect, behavioral, and health outcomes — each measures something different.

  • What outcomes will you measure at each level — and against what comparison?
  • On what timeline would each outcome be expected to show, given the theory you chose?
  • Which evaluation result would change your mind about whether to scale, shrink, or abandon the project?

The Build

Lens: Institutional intrapreneurship (Heinze & Weber, 2016)

This lens assumes that change is brokered. You work from where you are, with what's loose, through people who'll trade with you. It's opportunistic, incremental, and relational — and it pays close attention to whether the institutional ground beneath you is firm enough to hold the next step. Hold your project up to this lens and answer in your own words.

Reference How this framework works

Heinze and Weber studied how change agents inside institutions introduce practices the dominant logic doesn't favor — without being expelled and without converting everyone first. Their case studies of integrative-medicine programs surface the pieces that distinguish projects that took root from projects that didn't. The prompts below ask after each of those pieces. Use this reference if a construct feels unfamiliar.

Free space
A pocket of organizational slack where you can try something without first asking permission of the people most likely to refuse. Without free space, every step depends on convincing a gatekeeper. With it, you can produce evidence before you have to argue.
Field-level status
The standing of your idea in the broader professional field — not at your institution, but across the discipline. Higher status means colleagues listen; lower status means every conversation starts from defense. Status shifts come from accreditation language, payer policy, professional society statements, funding-agency activity, and public demand.
Jurisdiction over resources
For every resource your project needs — budget, time, space, curriculum slots, EHR fields, names on letterhead — someone has the authority to grant it. Some of those someones are you; most are not. Knowing which is which determines what you can act on alone and what requires a negotiation.
Trading zones
Places where you and stakeholders from a different logic exchange value without either side having to convert. A co-authored protocol, a joint pilot, a shared dataset, a committee — each anchors a trade in something concrete enough that the exchange survives past the meeting.
Pipeline of allies
Allies come in layers. Early adopters already half-believe and will say yes first. Bridging figures have credibility in both your world and the dominant one — they translate. Silent beneficiaries would gain if you succeeded but haven't spoken up yet; activating them is its own project.
First small experiment
The smallest visible action that produces a result someone can see. Its job isn't to prove the whole concept — it's to generate evidence, recruit the next ally, and keep the failure cost low enough that you can adjust.
Reinterpreting institutional logics (Reay et al., 2017)
When direct persuasion fails, the same project can sometimes be retold in the listener's own language and values — and assented to without anyone feeling they've abandoned their identity. The art is reframing honestly: serving their logic without becoming something you're not.

For depth: Heinze, K. L., & Weber, K. (2016). Toward organizational pluralism: Institutional intrapreneurship in integrative medicine. Organization Science, 27(1), 157–172. Optional companion for the reframing construct: Reay, T., et al. (2017). Getting leopards to change their spots. Academy of Management Journal, 60(3), 1043–1070.

1

Free space

A pocket of organizational slack where you can experiment without immediate scrutiny from those most likely to resist.

  • Where inside your institution could you actually try a small version of this — without requiring permission from a stakeholder who would say no?
  • What slack is available to you — discretionary time, a sympathetic department, a teaching clinic, a budget line, square footage, an unused protocol?
  • Whose attention does that space currently not attract — and is that protective or fragile?

Field-level status

The standing of integrative health in your professional field right now — and the direction it's moving.

  • How would a skeptical colleague describe integrative health's standing in your field today? Contested? Peripheral? Emerging? Accepted?
  • What recent shifts — accreditation language, payer policy, society statements, NIH center activity, public demand — raise or lower that standing?
  • Which of those shifts can you point to in a conversation tomorrow — by name, by source?

3

Jurisdiction over resources

For each resource your project needs, someone decides. Sometimes that someone is you.

  • List the resources your project needs — budget lines, faculty time, clinical hours, curriculum slots, EHR fields, space, names on a letterhead.
  • For each one: who controls it? Where do you already have jurisdiction? Where will you have to negotiate or borrow it?
  • Which resource is the bottleneck — the one that, if you couldn't get it, would stop everything?

Your check-in

4

Trading zones

Places where people from different logics — biomedicine, finance, administration, integrative health — exchange resources or legitimacy without either side having to convert.

  • What would the other side get from trading with you? A metric they need? A grant they can't write alone? A patient experience score?
  • Where could the exchange take place — a committee, a co-authored protocol, a joint pilot, a shared dataset, a single shared meeting?
  • What concrete artifact would anchor the trade so it doesn't dissolve when the meeting ends?

Your check-in

5

Pipeline of allies

Early adopters, bridging figures with credibility in both worlds, and silent beneficiaries.

  • Who are your early adopters — the people who already half-believe and would say yes first?
  • Who are your bridging figures — people credible to both the integrative-health world and the dominant one?
  • Who would benefit if this succeeded, even if they're silent now? What would activate them?
  • Who is missing from your pipeline — and what kind of person is missing, not just which name?

Your check-in

6

The first small experiment

The smallest visible thing you can do in the next 30 days that produces a result you can show someone.

  • What is that smallest visible action? Whose permission do you actually need, and whose don't you?
  • What result would it produce — and who needs to see that result for the next step to open?
  • What would you do if it worked? What would you do if it didn't, and how would you tell the difference?

Your check-in

Optional: Reinterpreting the institutional logic (Reay et al., 2017)

When direct persuasion fails, sometimes you can shift buy-in by retelling the same project in the listener's own language and values.

  • Whose buy-in are you actually trying to shift? What story do they currently tell about integrative health, in their own words?
  • What is their dominant logic — efficiency, safety, evidence, patient experience, professional autonomy, fiscal stewardship? Where does your project already serve that logic without losing what it is?
  • What reframing would let them say yes without feeling they've abandoned their identity?
  • Where is the line between honest reframing and selling something you didn't bring?

Why it Stalled

Diagnostic beat — drawing on Heinze & Weber's nursing-faculty case

Use this on a project that ran out of fuel — one of yours from a past role, a colleague's, or a case from the literature. It's not meant for the project you're currently launching. It's a way to learn from a fragile or failed effort before your own meets the same pattern. The questions are unflattering on purpose. They're also private.

Plan ≈ 15–25 minutes. Best after you've spent some time in the other two lenses. 0 / 6
Reference The case behind this diagnostic

Heinze and Weber describe an integrative-health nursing program that launched with substantial grant funding but had not built two of the things that carry a project past its first funder: free space independent of the grant, and field-level status that would have made integrative health a recognized line of work in nursing. When the funding cycle ended, there was no home left for the work and no allies whose own interests required them to defend it. The program dissolved.

The case is useful not as a story of failure but as a check on a familiar pattern: a project that looks robust because it is well-resourced but is in fact resting on a single thread. The prompts below pull that thread on a project of your choosing — yours, a colleague's, or a historical case — to surface where the institutional ground was firm and where it wasn't.

Source: Heinze, K. L., & Weber, K. (2016). Toward organizational pluralism: Institutional intrapreneurship in integrative medicine. Organization Science, 27(1), 157–172.
